The 12 Principles — tap each to reflect
1
Sincerity — approach for the sake of Allah (SWT)
›

Begin every session with a sincere intention. The goal is not academic knowledge alone, but drawing closer to Allah (SWT). Let this shape the atmosphere from the first moment.

Pause & ask tonightCheck in silently: what brought you to this circle? Renew that intention before you open the Quran.
2
Reality of the Quran — approach it as a cure
›

The scholar asked us to think of ourselves as a sick patient who craves the Quran as medicine. This is not metaphor — it is an orientation of the heart.

Pause & ask tonightWhat ails you right now — in your life, your character, your relationship with Allah? Come to these verses with that need.
3
Physical copies — honour the sanctity of His word
›

Reading from a physical Mushaf focuses the mind and manifests the sanctity of Allah's word more powerfully than a screen. It is a simple act with a deep spiritual dimension.

ReminderEnsure everyone in the group has a physical copy tonight. If not, pair up.
4
Recitation — fill the session with barakah
›

Begin by reciting the verses aloud. This is not a formality — the very words of Allah (SWT) carry barakah and set the tone for everything that follows.

TonightRecite slowly. Let the group follow in their copies. Hold a moment of silence after the recitation before anyone speaks.
5
Translation — an honest starting point
›

Read the translation during or after recitation to grasp the apparent meaning. Acknowledge openly that translations lose depth — but they are the doorway, not the destination.

Remind the groupTranslation gives us a map, not the territory. Keep that humility as we go deeper.
6
Word by word — the depth is in the details
›

Examine each word carefully. Arabic is a language where a single root carries layers of meaning. One word can open an entire theological universe.

TonightPause on every key term. Write them on a board or paper. What concepts orbit each word?
7
Tadabbur — contemplate and question
›

Sit with the verse. What does it say to you? Write down questions that arise naturally as you read. The act of writing crystallises thought and opens new understanding.

TonightGive the group 5 minutes of silent writing before discussion. See the Tadabbur tab for guiding questions.
8
No forced conclusions — hold ideas, verify later
›

Discussion is not about arriving at answers. It is about honest inquiry. Pose questions, explore possibilities, but hold conclusions loosely — then verify with scholars and tafsirs.

Group normEnd each discussion point with "let's note this to verify" rather than "so the meaning is..."
9
Choosing tafsir — match the level to the group
›

Some tafsirs are too surface-level to add depth. Others assume technical prerequisites you may not yet have — and can lead to misunderstanding. Choose one that stretches the group without overwhelming it.

ConsiderAre the tafsirs being presented tonight at the right level? Could you add a second source for comparison?
10
Gradual process — one step at a time
›

Everyone can understand the Quran to some degree. Aim first for the apparent meanings. More will open with time, patience, and continued study. Do not rush the journey.

Encouragement18 months and now 11 people — that is itself a sign of sincere effort. Trust the process.
11
Eternal message — revelation speaks to now
›

Even verses revealed in specific historical moments carry universal and timeless guidance. Every person in this room tonight can find something that speaks directly to their life.

TonightBefore closing, invite each person to share one thing from the verses they are taking home with them.
12
Ahlul Bayt (a) — the living Quran
›

The Ahlul Bayt (a) are the true embodiments of the Quran. A genuine understanding requires their words and explanations. Hadith from the Imams (a) are not supplementary — they are foundational.

Always askWhat have the Ahlul Bayt (a) said about this verse? This should be a core part of every session.

How to lead a topic — the framework
  • 1
    Select your ayah(s) or concept
    Be specific. A single ayah explored deeply is worth more than five covered superficially. If covering a concept, anchor it to specific ayat.
  • 2
    Linguistic analysis
    Look for repetition, specific word choices, and the order of subjects. Ask: why this word and not another? Is there a pattern or emphasis? Consult Arabic lexicons if possible.
  • 3
    In-depth meaning
    What is the verse actually saying at a deeper level? Move beyond the translation. What theological, ethical, or spiritual realities does it point to?
  • 4
    Asbab al-Nuzul — context of revelation
    What was the situation when this ayah was revealed? Understanding the historical and prophetic context illuminates meaning — but remember, the message is eternal and universal.
  • 5
    Tafsir — read multiple sources
    Do not rely on a single tafsir. Bring at least two. Where they agree, that is well-established ground. Where they differ, that is where the richest discussion begins.
  • 6
    Hadith of the Ahlul Bayt (a)
    Include narrations from the Imams (a) as a foundational element. What have they said about this verse or concept? This is not optional in a Shia tafsir circle.
  • 7
    How does this affect me today?
    Bring it home. How does this verse speak to your daily life, your character, your relationships, your community? Invite the group to reflect, not just listen.

Adding depth — practical tips

  • Present the Arabic root of key words
  • Compare two tafsirs side by side
  • Read a related hadith before the tafsir section
  • Use silence — give the group time to sit with the verse
  • End with an open question, not a conclusion

What causes sessions to lose depth

  • Reading only one tafsir superficially
  • Rushing through verses to cover more
  • Skipping the linguistic dimension entirely
  • Not including hadith from the Ahlul Bayt (a)
  • Presenting without connecting to daily life
Eight questions for deep contemplation
Question 1
What does Allah (SWT) want me to feel?
Question 2
Why this word and not another?
Question 3
Who is being addressed — and does it include me?
Question 4
What action does this verse call me to?
Question 5
What question does this raise? Write it down.
Question 6
How does this verse relate to those around it?
Question 7
What would the Imam (a) say to me through this verse?
Question 8
Where do I see this verse in the world today?
